How to Develop a True Growth Mindset

October 15, 2025 By Sarah Jenkins 7 min read

Think about a time you faced a difficult challenge and ultimately failed. Did you tell yourself, "I'm just not smart enough for this," or did you think, "I need to approach this differently or try harder"? The difference between those two reactions lies entirely in your underlying mindset.

Coined by Stanford psychologist Dr. Carol Dweck, the term "growth mindset" refers to the belief that human capacities—like intelligence, talent, and abilities—are not fixed traits, but rather malleable qualities that can be cultivated through effort, learning, and persistence.

The Difference: Fixed vs. Growth Mindset

Individuals with a Fixed Mindset believe that their intelligence and talents are static givens. They spend their time documenting their intelligence or talent instead of developing them. They also believe that talent alone creates success—without effort.

Conversely, people with a Growth Mindset thrive on challenge and see failure not as evidence of un-intelligence but as a heartening springboard for growth and for stretching our existing abilities. They understand that no one has ever accomplished truly great things—not Mozart, not Darwin, not Michael Jordan—without years of passionate practice and learning.

"In a growth mindset, challenges are exciting rather than threatening. So rather than thinking, oh, I'm going to reveal my weaknesses, you say, wow, here's a chance to grow." — Carol Dweck

1. The Power of "Yet"

One of the simplest yet most effective ways to trigger a mindset shift is to add the word "yet" to the end of your negative statements. When your internal monologue says, "I don't know how to code," or "I'm not good at public speaking," you are making a definitive, fixed statement about your identity.

Instead, reframe it: "I don't know how to code... yet." "I am not good at public speaking... yet." This single word opens up a timeline into the future, indicating that you are on a learning curve and that mastery is simply a matter of time and effort.

2. Embracing the Role of Failure

In a fixed mindset, failing at a task means you are a failure. It is a direct assault on your ego. In a growth mindset, failing at a task simply means that the specific strategy you employed didn't work. It provides data.

To cultivate a growth mindset, you must divorce your self-worth from the outcome. Start actively asking yourself after every setback: "What did I learn from this? What can I do differently next time?" By analyzing failure objectively, it ceases to be something to fear and becomes a natural part of the scientific method of success.

3. Praising Process Over Outcome

Society conditions us to praise outcomes: the A+ on the test, the promotion, the gold medal. However, if you want to foster a growth mindset within yourself (or within your children and team members), you must learn to praise the process.

Instead of saying, "You got an A because you are so smart," say, "You got an A because you studied consistently for three weeks and used effective flashcard systems." By praising the strategy, the effort, and the persistence, you reinforce the behaviors that lead to success, rather than a fixed identity.

Conclusion

A growth mindset is not something you either have or don't have; it is a muscle that must be continually exercised. Whenever you catch yourself avoiding a challenge, feeling threatened by the success of others, or giving up easily, recognize that your fixed mindset is steering the ship. Patiently redirect your thoughts, apply the word "yet," and focus entirely on the process of becoming better.
